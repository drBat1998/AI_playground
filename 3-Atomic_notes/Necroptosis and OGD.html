Introduction

Stroke is an important cause of death and disability, alongside heart and oncological diseases, all around the globe. For decades only approved treatment in this case has been tissue plasminogen. Unfortunately, it has a narrow therapeutic window and a bunch of complications. Therefore, it is extremely important to find new treatment strategies and validate them in vitro and in vivo.

Oxygen-glucose deprivation and reperfusion (OGD/R) is a well-known in vitro model for studying ischemic diseases, including but not limited to stroke[1], retinal ischemia-reperfusion injury[2], spinal cord injury[3], glaucoma[4]

Intrinsic apoptosis, the most studied cell death type, plays an important role in neuronal death during I/R injury. However, the complete blocking of caspase rescue significantly number of neurons, but not all cells, from death.[5] This suggests that some other cell death may play a role during ischemia-reperfusion injury. Xu et al. showed that it is a case by increasing neuron survival during OGD/R due to the effect of necrostatin 1, a RIPK1 inhibitor, however, they did not explicitly state the cell death type.

In addition, during a stroke, damaged neurons initiate microglia activation. Well-known molecules such as NFkB and p38 promote TNFα secretion by microglia. This TNFα may lead to external apoptosis pathway initiation in "healthy neurons"[6].

Necroptosis

Necroptosis is a form of regulated cell death (RCD) that manifests morphologically as necrosis by cell swelling and permeabilization of the cellular membrane. Nomenclature Committee on Cell Death in 2018 proposed to define necroptosis as a type of RCD triggered by extracellular or intracellular perturbation of homeostasis that depends on MLKL and RIPK3 kinase activity[7]. Different death receptors, including TNFR1, TLR3, TLR4, and ZBP1, detect the perturbation and initiate RIPK3 activity. Active RIPK3 phosphorylates MLKL, resulting in the formation of MLKL oligomers and triggering membrane permeabilization and Ca influx.

TNFR1 engagement in necroptosis is best characterized so far. TNF connection with the receptor may initiate three pathways. First, the most common is RIPK1-dependent NF-kb pathway activation that promotes cell survival and inflammation response by initiating Complex I. Complex 1 consists of TNF-receptor-associated factor 2, RIPK1, cIAP1, cIAP2, and LUBAC. cIAPs and LUBAC promote RIPK1 ubiquitination. This leads to the creation of a platform that promotes NF-kb and MAPK pathway activation. Disruption of complex I leads to forming complex IIa or IIb. The second pathway, mediated by complex IIa, initiates apoptosis by CASP8. Caspase 8 inhibits apoptosis in most cases. Thus, in the absence of CASP8 activity, any ligands of TNFR1, TLR3, or TLR4 promote complex IIb (necrosome) assembly and execution of the cell by necroptosis.

Moreover, Feng et al. claimed that the p38MAPK/CYLD pathway is involved in the promotion of necroptosis in primary cortical neurons during ischemia. [8]

OGD

Meloni et al. showed interesting observations related to cell death during the oxygen-glucose deprivation model. They showed that necrostatin 1 showed a small influence on the cell survival rate. They suggest that it is due to a lack of necroptosis if the cells are predominantly neurons due to a lack of glial cells and, respectivly lack of TNFa activation of the necroptosis pathway[9]. In addition, Fan et al. [3-1] argued that MLKL, the key executor of necroptosis, in the context of spinal cord injury, is expressed mainly in microglia and astrocytes.

In order to study necroptosis in the context of stroke, researchers use inhibitors of caspases such as zVAD[10]. As expected, LDH assay didn't show significant differences between oxygen glucose deprivation groups with and without zVAD.

In the context of OGD, many molecules showed neuroprotective roles by inhibition of necroptosis, including: tubacin(a specific HDAC6 inhibitor)[11], RIPK1-inhibitory compound (RIC)[4-1], SB203580 (a p38 MAPK inhibitor)[8-1]
