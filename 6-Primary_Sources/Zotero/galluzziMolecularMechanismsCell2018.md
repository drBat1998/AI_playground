### Ideas, thoughts, useful information

> [!quote]+ Note ([Page ](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=486&annotation=8KQTH5N2))
  **Accidental cell death is instantaneous and catastrophic demise of cells exposed to severe insults.**

[[accidental cell death]]

> [!quote]+ Note ([Page ](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=486&annotation=RGBHUERY))
  **Regulated cell death is not unique to multicellular life forms, program that eliminate cell in response to intracellular or extracellular microenvironment changes, in some way prioritise general evolution than individual one.**

[[programmed cell death in bacteria]]

> [!quote]+ Note ([Page 2](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=487&annotation=GL89KR56))
  **Morphologically cell death could be divided into three groups. Type 1 - exhibiting sytoplasmic shrinkage, pyknosis, karyorrhexis, and plasma membrane blebbing. Type 2 manifesting by excesive cytoplasmic vacuolization, increasing phaocytic uptake and lysosomal degradation. Type 3 displayin disposal of cell corpses in the absense of obvious phagocytic and lysosomal involvement.**

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 7](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=492&annotation=PGMWVR4M))
> NCCD proposes to define intrinsic apoptosis as a form of RCD initiated by perturbations of the intracellular or extracellular microenvironment, demarcated by MOMP and precipitated by executioner caspases, mainly CASP3 

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 11](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=496&annotation=EAVLIFM9))
> MPT-driven necrosis as a form of RCD triggered by perturbations of the intracellular microenvironment and relying on CYPD 

[[necroptosis]]
> [!quote]+ Note ([Page 11](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=496&annotation=CRED4M5D))
  **Necroptosis initiated by FAS, TNFR1, pathogen recognition receptors such as TLR3, TLR4, and Z-DNA binding protein 1.**

> [!quote]+ Note ([Page 11](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=496&annotation=MPK35BEB))
  **RIPK3 and MLKL are esential molecular components of necroptosis. RIPK3 activation by RIPK1 or other kinase promote MLKL phosphorylation, resulting in the formation of oligomer that translocate to the plasma membrane, where they bin specific phosphtidylinositol phosphate species.**

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 11](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=496&annotation=9I6JB6I5))
> Alternatively, RIPK3 can be activated following the RHIM-dependent interaction with (1) TRIF upon either TLR3 activation by double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) within endosomes, or TLR4 activation by lipopolysaccharide (LPS) or various DAMPs at the plasma membrane [428]; or (2) ZBP1, which operates as a sensor for cytosolic DNApromoting type I interferon (IFN) synthesis and NF-κB activation [439–441]. 

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 12](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=497&annotation=CMX5FAA5))
> MLKL oligomerization promotes a cascade of intracellular events involving (1) Ca2+ influx, which is presumably mediated by the MLKL target transient receptor potential cation channel subfamily M member 7 (TRPM7) [449]; and (2) PS exposure, which seems to be directly operated by MLKL 

> [!quote]+ Note ([Page 12](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=497&annotation=E7NPGWBZ))
  **MLKL also promote Ca influx through TRPM7, and PS exposure.**

> [!quote]+ Note ([Page 12](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=497&annotation=5V886HKT))
  **Pseudonecrosis has been describe by involving just MLKL or RIPK3**

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 12](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=497&annotation=9R2M43AI))
> Major negative regulators of the necrosome include: (1) STIP1 homology and U-box containing protein 1 (STUB1; also known as CHIP), which promotes RIPK1 and RIPK3 ubiquitination followed by lysosomal degradation [472, 473]; (2) A20, which inhibits necrosome assembly by deubiquitinating RIPK3 [473, 474]; (3) protein phosphatase, Mg2+/Mn2+ dependent 1B (PPM1B), which prevents MLKL recruitment to the necrosome by dephosphorylating RIPK3 [475]; and (4) aurora kinase A (AURKA), which mediates inhibitory function upon physical interaction with RIPK1 and RIPK3 [476]. 

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 12](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=497&annotation=IKUMFNAS))
> In addition, the assembly of the necrosome upon TNFR1 stimulation impinges on two conditions: (1) pharmacological or genetic CASP8 inactivation [478, 479], and (2) RIPK1 deubiquitination-dependent phosphorylation (at least in some settings), which can be favored by exogenously provided SMAC mimetics, ensuring the release of RIPK1 from complex I (see above) [334, 335, 480, 481]. 

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 12](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=497&annotation=E5VKQA6D))
> Necroptosis is also tonically inhibited by c-IAPs, owing to their ability to ubiquitinate RIPK1 [490–493]. 

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 13](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=498&annotation=IGE3ATE4))
> Collectively, these results suggest that (at least in some settings) RIPK1 can inhibit (rather than activate) RIPK3-dependent necroptosis and/or CASP8dependent extrinsic apoptosis [504]. At least in some settings, this reflects the major role of RIPK1 in NF-κB activation [505–507]. 

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 13](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=498&annotation=DK8EBTS3))
> Thus, in the absence of CASP8 activity, stimulation of TLR3 or TLR4 by their respective ligands promotes necroptosis upon the interaction between TRIF and RIPK3 and the consequent activation of MLKL [428]. 

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 13](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=498&annotation=NNQHX72G))
> ZBP1 acts at the initial steps of necroptosis by mediating the sequential activation of RIPK3 and MLKL. 

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 13](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=498&annotation=XG74F7S2))
> Importantly, multiple components of the molecular machinery for necroptosis—including ZBP1, RIPK3, MLKL, and TNFR1 (mainly via NF-κB)—impinge on the control of the so-called “inflammasome”, a supramolecular platform for the activation of CASP1 and consequent secretion of mature interleukin 1 beta (IL1B; best known as IL-1β) and IL18 [524–529]. Discussing in detail these links —which exemplify the complex interconnection between RCD signaling and inflammatory responses—goes beyond the scope of this review [4, 8 530–532]. 

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 14](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=499&annotation=JYYKTLCB))
> we propose to define necroptosis as a type of RCD triggered by perturbations of extracellular or intracellular homeostasis that critically depends on MLKL, RIPK3, and (at least in some settings) on the kinase activity of RIPK1 

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page 18](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=503&annotation=L3B6WA8J))
> parthanatos as a form of RCD initiated by PARP1 hyperactivation and precipitated by the consequent bioenergetic catastrophe coupled to AIFdependent and MIF-dependent DNA degradation 
### Questions, things that I didn't understand

> [!quote]+ Highlight ([Page ](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/VCNLFB6P?page=486&annotation=QAPGRVCM))
> Intriguingly, regulated cell death (RCD) is not unique to multicellular life forms, a setting in which RCD has an obvious advantage for organismal homeostasis in both physiological and pathological settings [5–9],
